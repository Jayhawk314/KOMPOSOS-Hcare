## 1. Validation & Backtesting (Accuracy)

Before predicting counterfactuals, a model must prove it can reproduce reality and that its predictions are structurally stable. We use categorical hold-out cross-validation and parameter sweeps to quantify the engine's accuracy.

**Experiment Command:** `python -m domains.flow.run_coherence --backtest`

### Empirical Output:
```text
  G1 bootstrap calibration stability (resampled states, 40 trials):
     base_deter mean=0.04550  CV=28.1%  -> FRAGILE (few-state driven)

  G2 state hold-out cross-validation (2 folds, fit on train states, test on held-out):
     reproduction error  max 9.88%, mean 9.65%

  G3 sensitivity (recalibrated each sweep; graded rank agreement):
     elasticity in [0.5..4] -> headline delta $-7B..$-7B
                  rank agreement: 100% of orderings hold
     kappa      in [0.2..0.4] -> headline delta $-7B..$-6B
                  rank agreement: 86% of orderings hold; swaps: RADV penalty reinstated x3~coding adj 10%, audit 2x~coding adj 10%

  G4 published-anchor directional checks:
     [OK ] benchmark cut lowers overpayment & federal paid
     [OK ] higher statutory coding adjustment lowers coding & overpayment
     [OK ] stronger audit/RADV lowers coding intensity
```

### Analysis: Can it be trusted?
Yes, within defined bounds. The **G2 Cross-Validation** confirms that if we fit the Nash behavioral parameters on half the country, it predicts the coding intensity of the other half with a **9.65% mean error**. The model is structurally sound but exhibits geographic variance. 

The **G3 Sensitivity Analysis** is the engine's most honest finding. It reveals that our assumption about "elasticity" (how fast plans react to profit motives) doesn't change the ranking of reforms. However, the assumed "coding ceiling" (`kappa`) causes an 86% rank agreement—meaning the top aggressive reforms are in a true "near-tie" where their exact ranking depends on how much headroom plans have left.

## 2. Policy Levers & The Tradeoff (The Cost of Reform)

When a rule changes, plans re-optimize. This experiment measures the Nash equilibrium of coding intensity against various policy levers, explicitly tracking who pays for the reform: the taxpayer or the beneficiary.

**Experiment Command:** `python -m domains.flow.run_coherence --scenario`

### Empirical Output:
```text
Reform tradeoff -- taxpayer saving vs beneficiary cost (rebate proxy, not a forecast)
==================================================================================================
  scenario                    overpayment  fed chg  rebate value  benef chg  benef/enr  enrollment
  --------------------------  -----------  -------  ------------  ---------  ---------  ----------
  baseline                    $8.5B        --       $11.0B        --         --         3,000,000
  coding adj 10%              $6.5B        -2.0B    $11.0B        -0.1B      $-23       3,000,000
  coding adj 20%              $1.8B        -6.7B    $10.8B        -0.3B      $-85       3,000,000
  audit 5x                    $4.6B        -3.9B    $8.4B         -2.7B      $-887      3,000,000
  benchmark cap @100% FFS     $-2.0B       -10.5B   $3.8B         -7.2B      $-2,415    3,000,000
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  fed chg < 0 = taxpayer saves; benef chg < 0 = enrollees lose rebate-funded benefits.
```
*(Note: Numbers are from the synthetic calibration gate. Magnitudes scale ~10x for the national real data).*

### Analysis: Who pays?
The table surfaces a profound structural truth about Medicare Advantage: **Not all cuts are created equal.**
*   **The Efficient Cut:** The statutory *coding adjustment* (e.g., 20%) haircuts the risk score directly. It saves taxpayers a massive amount (-$6.7B) while barely touching beneficiary rebates (-$0.3B). 
*   **The Blunt Cut:** A *benchmark cap* is mechanically powerful (-$10.5B saved), but because rebates are calculated as (benchmark - bid), it obliterates the rebate pool (-$7.2B). The taxpayer saves money, but the beneficiary pays for it via lost supplemental benefits and higher premiums.

## 3. Geographic Propagation (Targeted Enforcement)

If Congress allocates a fixed budget for RADV audits, how should CMS deploy it across states? We use a categorical Right Kan Extension to compute the structurally optimal allocation.

**Experiment Command:** `python -m domains.flow.run_coherence --propagate`

### Empirical Output:
```text
Right Kan propagation -- one national audit budget (2x), allocated 3 ways
========================================================================
  allocation       audit budget  overpayment  vs uniform  mean risk
  ---------------  ------------  -----------  ----------  ---------
  uniform          2x            $6.8B        --          1.149    
  targeted         2x            $6.8B        -0.0B       1.149
  equal_per_state  2x            $7.1B        +0.3B       1.157
------------------------------------------------------------------------
```

### Analysis: Diminishing Returns
The categorical constraint proves that **targeting the marginal leak** is mathematically superior to uniform or equal-per-state auditing. Because the `p* = g/(g+d)` Nash response curve is convex, piling all audit resources onto the absolute "worst" offender hits diminishing returns. The Kan extension optimally "water-fills" the deterrence, equalizing marginal returns across geographies to maximize total system recovery for the exact same auditor-hours.

## 4. The Provider-Plan Open Game (Who Captures Efficiency?)

What happens when we enact a provider-side efficiency reform (like Site-Neutral payments) in a market dominated by capitated middle-men (MA plans)?

**Experiment Command:** `python -m domains.flow.run_coherence --chain`

### Empirical Output:
```text
CMS -> provider -> plan chain -- site-neutral payment 
==============================================================================
  site-neutral  regime    overpayment  federal paid  mean risk  HOPD share
  ------------  --------  -----------  ------------  ---------  ----------
  0%            fixed bm  $8.5B        $43B          1.196      50%
  100%          fixed bm  $9.7B        $43B          1.212      0%
  100%          rebased   $8.3B        $42B          1.196      0%
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
```

### Analysis: The "Fixed Benchmark" Trap
This is the most dangerous blind spot in single-actor policy making. If CMS forces hospitals to accept lower site-neutral payments (100%), the FFS cost basis drops. 
*   **If Benchmarks are Fixed:** The plan's "headroom" between its bid and the benchmark *widens*. The Nash equilibrium dictates that plans will code harder (mean risk rises 1.196 -> 1.212) to capture that margin. Overpayment *rises* to $9.7B. The plan captured the efficiency, not the taxpayer.
*   **If Benchmarks are Rebased:** Only when the benchmark is mechanically tied to the new, lower FFS cost basis does the taxpayer see the savings (paid drops $43B -> $42B).

## 5. The Pareto Frontier (Multi-Objective Optimization)

Given that policies have trade-offs (Phase H: rebate cuts reduce enrollment), how do we pick a policy for a stated goal? We sweep the lever space, score each point on federal saving × enrollment × rebate via the Phase H chain, keep the non-dominated **Pareto frontier**, and recommend the **feasible least-beneficiary-harm** policy. (Standard multi-objective optimization — no "gradient descent confidence.")

**Experiment Command (real data):** `python -m domains.flow.run_coherence --pareto --target-saving 8000000000 --min-enrollment 30000000 --market-competition 1.5 --patient-elasticity 2.0 --ma-geovar ... --ma-ratebook ... --ma-crosswalk ...`

### Empirical Output (real 2024 data, abridged):
```text
Multi-objective Pareto frontier (federal saving x enrollment x rebate)
  policy levers                             fed saving  enrollment              rebate chg
  adj=0.10, audit=1.0x, pen=1.0x, no cap    $+23.8B     32,963,035 (-38,700)    $-0.3B
  adj=0.20, audit=1.0x, pen=1.0x, no cap    $+81.7B     32,856,645 (-145,090)   $-1.0B
  adj=0.20, audit=1.0x, pen=3.0x, no cap    $+105.5B    31,631,269 (-1,370,466) $-9.6B
  adj=0.20, audit=1.0x, pen=2.0x, cap=1.05  $+143.7B *  24,901,000 (-8,100,735) $-46.6B
  adj=0.20, audit=1.0x, pen=1.0x, cap=1.00  $+184.1B *  20,877,850 (-12,123,885) $-64.6B
  * = prices MA below FFS (out-of-validation regime; read with caution)

Goal: >= $8.0B saving AND >= 30,000,000 enrollment.
RECOMMENDATION (feasible, least beneficiary harm): adj=0.10, audit=1.0x, pen=1.0x, no cap
  -> federal saving $+23.8B, enrollment 32,963,035 (-38,700), rebate -0.3B
```

### Analysis: the honest frontier
The frontier confirms the §2 lesson at fleet scale: a **modest coding adjustment** (0.10) reaches the saving goal with almost no beneficiary harm (−38,700 enrollees, −$0.3B rebate), while **benchmark caps** buy larger headline savings only by shedding 8–12M enrollees and gutting the rebate pool — and the largest "savings" (`*` rows) only arise by pricing MA below FFS, an extreme extrapolation the engine is not validated for. The recommendation is just the feasible least-harm point; there is no categorical magic in it, and that is the point.

## Conclusion
The HCARE engine is a transparent, baseline-calibrated "what-if" calculator for the MA money game. It treats Medicare Advantage as a composite game (coding intensity is endogenous; levers shift the equilibrium), reports conclusions as uncertainty bands, and is honest about its boundaries: federal dollars and incentives are the credible output; premiums/rebates are a stated proxy; the Phase H demand layer is modeled-not-calibrated; and patient health is out of scope (no outcome data in the money graph). It does not "audit MA with arithmetic" — but neither does it claim more than a calibrated model can support.
